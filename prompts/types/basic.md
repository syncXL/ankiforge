### BASIC

Description:
A two-sided card where the front presents a prompt and the back contains the answer.
Direction: prompt → answer. Term → definition. Concept → explanation. Question → answer.
Used for direct recall — the student sees the front and must retrieve the back.

When to use:
- Vocabulary, definitions, named concepts, formulas, classifications.
- One-to-one mappings: one prompt, one clear answer.
- High-volume recall practice designed for spaced repetition.
- When the student needs to go from hearing or seeing a term to knowing what it means.

When NOT to use:
- Complex multi-part concepts that cannot be reduced to a clean front/back pair — split them.
- When the back requires more than 3 sentences to be complete — split the concept.
- Procedural knowledge with many steps — steps do not compress cleanly into a back face.

Answer structure:
- FRONT: the prompt. A term, a question, a concept label.
  Must be specific enough that only one answer is correct.
  ✅ "What is the function of the mitochondria?"
  ❌ "What does a cell do?" — too broad, multiple valid answers
- BACK: the answer. A definition, explanation, or value.
  Concise — maximum 3 sentences. If more is needed, the card is too complex.
  Must directly and completely answer the front. No tangents.
  Stage 2 will use FRONT and BACK to generate the hint and tags.

Flashcard writing techniques:
- One concept per card. If a concept needs more than one front/back pair, generate multiple cards.
- Do not copy text verbatim from the source material. Paraphrase.
  Verbatim extraction tests phrasing recognition, not concept understanding.
- The front must not contain the answer or any part of the answer.
- The back must draw only from the source material.

Output format:
FRONT: <prompt>
BACK: <answer — maximum 3 sentences>
TYPE: basic

Example:
FRONT: What is osmosis?
BACK: The movement of water molecules across a semipermeable membrane from an area of
      low solute concentration to an area of high solute concentration.
TYPE: basic
